April 2018

24th

DH said that there are some researchers who relate the volatility anomaly to distress preference. The researchers argue that investors regard stocks of distressed firms as unscratched lottery tickets and correspondingly overvalue them despite of their poor expected returns. It's reasonable at a glance as overcoming financial distress signals something positive with which a stock price surges up, but a little bit blurry as it employs a corporate finance rationale (if distressed) to account for an asset pricing phenomenon (if skewed).

23rd

This is the draft I submitted to Lubar School on April 10th. I sent it to Val to get some comments at that time and he sent me some feedback on April 13th. Roughly, he said that I should display some findings that weed out alternative explanations and narrow down the possibilities.

After receiving the comments, I tried to clearly state what I've done so far and the things I can do to further develop the paper. This is what I presented to Val and DH on April 19th and 20th, respectively. Their responses were not bad. Here are DH's comments.

  1. For the arbitrage portfolios, the alpha returns at \(t-11\), \(\cdots\), \(t-1\), \(t+1\), \(\cdots\), \(t+11\) as well as those at \(t\) should be reported.
  2. Are the volatilities persistent? Let's check using a transition matrix or something.
  3. Do momentum crashes (momentum due to underreaction when VIX is low, but reversal due to overreaction when VIX is high) exist? One can confirm the phenomena by estimating the average and alpha returns annually.
  4. The degree of skewness preference can vary over time. Do the average and alpha returns change as consumer and investor indices change?
  5. Are the findings robust? There is another decomposition method suggested by Chen, Da, and Zhao (2013).

And here are Val's comments.

  1. Roughly, there can be four volatilities, i.e. systematic and idiosyncratic volatilities of cash flow and discount rate shocks, respectively. It's reasonable for cash flow shocks to have idiosyncratic volatilities as investors frequently revise their expectation about future dividends, but do they often adjust corresponding discounts? Aren't the discount rate shocks systematic in nature? There should be a supporting example that shows the idiosyncratic discount rate shocks to bolster the motivation.
  2. Let's assume anyway that there exist idiosyncratic discount rate shocks. Does the decomposition method correctly identify cash flow and discount rate shocks and corresponding volatilities? What if there is a measurement error problem?

DH recommended me to submit the draft to the APAD conference, but I am unsure concerning the quality of this draft.
